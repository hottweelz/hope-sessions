Why Hope

The name came from the music. Not the other way around.

Paul Andrejack — leading the rock band that shares his surname, simply called Andrejack (the band is a distinct entity from Paul Andrejack the individual) — recorded an album called Hope. That’s where this initiative gets its name: from a real body of work that already existed, already had an audience, and already carried a creative identity. The initiative follows the music. That matters.

What does Hope Sessions actually build for artists who have no studio access?

The mission from there is concrete: use a working professional studio as something more than a private resource. Denver, NC has talented people who can’t access professional gear, can’t afford hourly studio rates, and don’t have relationships with experienced engineers or producers. Hope Sessions is built to change that math.

That means working with artists at every skill level — the experienced performer who’s never had the right room, the songwriter with a full notebook and no track record, the beginner who needs someone to explain the signal chain before the first session. None of that requires prior experience. It just requires showing up with something real to work on.

The new music being made here extends the artistic world of Hope. The access being built here extends the point of it. Neither one was invented for this application — both grew from years of actual work.

His studio in Denver, NC runs analog and digital consoles — Tascam DM-3200, Mackie, Peavey, Soundcraft — with outboard and processing from Focusrite, Avalon, Lexicon, TC Electronic, Slate, Waves, Pultec, and Fairchild.

What This Initiative Is Building

Start with strong new work. Build toward broader access.

Phase one is a focused recording project — new original songs, real finishing work, done properly. That’s the foundation everything else grows from.

01

New Work Recording

New original songs extending the world of Hope — tracked with discipline, finished to a real standard, released as completed work. Not demos shelved for later review, not rough cuts waiting for the right budget. Finished music that earns its place. The sessions opening Hope Sessions as a formal initiative are focused recording projects at PMA Recording Studios in Denver, NC: new songwriting by Paul Andrejack, production decisions made with the same discipline the album Hope was built with, and finished recordings ready for independent release. This phase establishes the creative and operational baseline for everything that follows — working proof that the studio produces releasable recordings at a professional level and that Paul Andrejack is making strong new work right now.

02

All-Level Artist Development

Working with artists wherever they are — first studio visit, first original song, or tenth album — to get rough ideas to something finished and release-ready. No assumed baseline. No shortcuts on the result. Artist development at Hope Sessions means different things for different artists: for a first-timer, it might mean understanding the signal chain, building a scratch track, and finding what the song actually needs before committing to a full session. For a more experienced performer, it means applying professional production and engineering judgment to work they already know is strong. What doesn’t change is the standard for the finished result — every artist leaves with something that can exist in the world, not a rough draft waiting for a better day.

03

Removing Barriers to Entry

Professional studio time and experienced guidance cost money most independent artists in Lincoln County don’t have. A single day of professional recording at a major-market studio runs several hundred dollars per session — before mixing, mastering, or any production costs are factored in. Grant support lets Hope Sessions remove that financial wall for selected artists, covering session time, equipment access, engineering hours, and production costs that would otherwise end the project before it starts. What determines who participates is the strength of the idea, not the size of the budget. That’s not a positioning statement — it’s a structural commitment backed by how the initiative is funded. The cost barrier is real, and removing it changes what’s possible for artists in this region.

04

Community Creative Infrastructure

A long-term model for connecting professional recording support to schools, nonprofits, and community organizations in Lincoln County. The kind of creative infrastructure that’s routine in cities like Charlotte or Raleigh — where students can access recording labs, arts programs, and working professionals through established institutional networks — is rare or nonexistent in Denver, NC. Hope Sessions is the foundation for building that infrastructure locally. In practice, this means after-school music production programs, workshop series, mentorship sessions with schools and faith communities, and story-centered music projects with nonprofits. The professional studio, the experience, and the relationships already exist here. This phase formalizes the community access model that converts those existing private resources into a public creative asset for Lincoln County.

05

Why Grants Fund This Work

Independent artists rarely have the resources to pay professional studio rates. A single recording session with a qualified engineer in a properly equipped room costs more than most working artists in Lincoln County can budget for a month of creative work — let alone for a full project requiring multiple sessions, mixing, mastering, and release preparation. Grant funding covers what they can’t: session time, equipment access, engineering and production support, and the artist development costs that make the difference between a strong idea staying rough and becoming a finished, releasable recording. Arts grants exist specifically to close this gap — converting professional infrastructure into community access. Hope Sessions is a direct match for that purpose, and the Arts & Science Council Lincoln County consortium is the right funding partner for this region.

06

Investing in Lincoln County Youth

Young people in Lincoln County deserve a reason to see a creative future here, close to home. Most youth in this region have never been inside a professional recording studio, spoken with a working producer or engineer, or had the chance to make original music in a real room with professional equipment. That gap has consequences: talented young people leave, or stop developing, or never discover a path that was available somewhere else all along. Hope Sessions is committed to programming that puts professional studio access, mentorship, and musical education directly in front of local youth — workshops, sessions, and educational collaborations with Lincoln County schools and community organizations giving the next generation something real to build toward inside the community they already call home.

Lincoln County Context

Professional creative infrastructure in a place that needs it.

Artists outside major market centers often have everything except the infrastructure to finish the work at a competitive level. Denver, North Carolina and Lincoln County sit outside the institutional networks of urban arts funding, recording access, and industry connections that artists in Charlotte, Raleigh, or Nashville can reach in 30 minutes.

The barriers are specific. Renting professional studio time elsewhere runs hundreds of dollars per session. Quality outboard gear isn’t something most people own. Experienced engineers and producers aren’t cheap or easy to find. For most working artists in this region, those costs end the project before it starts.

Hope Sessions is a response to that gap that starts from inside it. Paul already lives here, already runs a professional studio, already has the credentials to do the work. The question was never whether the infrastructure existed. It was whether it could serve more people than it currently does — and this initiative is the answer to that question.

Grant funding is what makes the access model viable. It covers the gap between what an independent artist can pay and what professional-quality recording actually costs — converting a private professional resource into a community one. That’s a direct match for what arts grants are built to support.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Hope Sessions?

Hope Sessions is a recording initiative led by Paul Andrejack at PMA Recording Studios in Denver, North Carolina. It begins with new original music extending the artistic world of the album Hope by the band Andrejack — and builds toward something broader: professional studio access, artist development, mentorship, and musical education for artists and youth in Lincoln County who currently have no path to it. The initiative is supported in part through an Artist Support Grant application to the Arts & Science Council Charlotte-Mecklenburg consortium for Lincoln County, with a September 2026 submission deadline.

Who is Paul Andrejack?

Paul Andrejack is a recording artist, producer, audio engineer, and studio owner based in Denver, North Carolina. He built his career on Long Island, New York — constructing his own studio, fronting and collaborating with multiple bands, recording and releasing albums, and completing multiple European tours as a working artist. He is an Internet Records recording artist who has co-written with Grammy Award winners Mark Mangold (Cher, Jordin Sparks) and Mark Gatz (The Cosby Show), collaborated with The Alessi Brothers (McCartney and Lennon collaborators), and consulted for Bakithi Kumalo (bassist for Paul Simon) and Kent Marcum (composer for Harpo Productions). He owns and operates PMA Recording Studios in Denver, NC.

How can artists participate in Hope Sessions?

Artists interested in participating can reach out through the contact form below or by emailing pmastudios@hotmail.com directly. No prior recording experience is required. Hope Sessions is designed for artists at every skill level — from first-time studio visitors and beginner songwriters to experienced performers who have never had access to a professional room. The standard for participation is the quality of the idea, not the length of an artist’s credits. Artists based in the Lincoln County or greater Denver, NC area are especially encouraged to reach out.

Can beginners or first-time studio visitors apply?

Yes. Hope Sessions is explicitly designed for artists at every skill level. Beginners, intermediate performers, and seasoned musicians are all welcome. You do not need prior studio experience, a recording history, or professional credits to participate. If you have original ideas — songs, concepts, or material you have been developing — and you are based in the Lincoln County or greater Denver, NC area, start a conversation. The only requirement is an honest one.

How can schools, nonprofits, or community organizations partner with Hope Sessions?

Schools, churches, nonprofits, arts organizations, and community groups in Lincoln County and the broader region can partner with Hope Sessions to bring professional studio access, music education, and artist development programming to their communities. Partnership can take many forms: after-school music production programs, workshops, mentorship sessions, or story-centered music projects. To start a conversation, contact Hope Sessions at pmastudios@hotmail.com or use the contact form below.
